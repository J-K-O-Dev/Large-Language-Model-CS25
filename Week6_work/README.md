### Week 6 Report: Aligning the Model with Human Preferences using DPO

So far, the project has focused on pretraining and supervised fine-tuning (SFT). While SFT is great for teaching a model a specific skill, it doesn't guarantee that the model's behavior aligns with what humans find helpful, truthful, or harmless. The next step in building a truly useful assistant is **preference tuning**. This week, I moved beyond SFT and explored a powerful and efficient alignment technique: **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)**.

#### The Theory: From Complex RLHF to Direct Optimization

The traditional method for model alignment is Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). RLHF is effective but notoriously complex, involving training a separate reward model and using reinforcement learning algorithms like PPO to tune the LLM. DPO offers a more direct and stable alternative.

- **The DPO Idea:** DPO recognizes that the end goal of RLHF is to increase the probability of preferred responses and decrease the probability of rejected ones. It cleverly reframes this objective into a simple binary classification loss that can be optimized directly on a preference dataset, completely bypassing the need for an explicit reward model and complex RL.

- **Core Components:** To implement DPO, you need three key things:
  1.  **A Preference Dataset:** This consists of triplets: a `prompt`, a `chosen` (preferred) response, and a `rejected` (dispreferred) response. I used the `Anthropic/hh-rlhf` dataset, which is a standard for this kind of work.
  2.  **The Base Model:** The model to be fine-tuned. Due to hardware constraints, I used the highly capable but smaller `TinyLlama/TinyLlama-1.1B-Chat-v1.0`.
  3.  **The Reference Model:** A copy of the base model that is kept frozen during training. The DPO loss function uses this as a baseline to measure how much the policy (the model being trained) is changing.

- **The DPO Loss and `beta`:** The DPO loss function simultaneously encourages the policy model to assign a higher likelihood to the `chosen` responses and a lower likelihood to the `rejected` responses compared to the reference model. The `beta` hyperparameter is crucial here; it controls how much the policy is allowed to deviate from the reference model. A low `beta` keeps the model close to its original behavior, while a high `beta` allows it to more aggressively fit the preference data.

#### Implementation: DPO with the TRL Library

The implementation, documented in `DPO_FINE_TUNING.IPYNB`, leverages the Hugging Face `trl` (Transformer Reinforcement Learning) library, which provides a convenient `DPOTrainer`.

- **Setup:** I loaded the `TinyLlama` model and tokenizer. A crucial step was creating two instances of the model: one to be trained (`model`) and an identical one to serve as the frozen `ref_model`.
- **Configuration:** I used the `DPOConfig` to set up the training arguments. This included setting a small batch size and using `paged_adamw_8bit` to manage memory usage on the Colab T4 GPU. I started with a `beta` of `0.3`.
- **Training:** The `DPOTrainer` was initialized with the models, config, and a small subset of the `hh-rlhf` dataset. The trainer API then handled the entire DPO training loop.
- **Analysis and Generation:** After training, I extracted and plotted the DPO-specific metrics logged by the trainer, including the loss, rewards for chosen/rejected responses, and the reward margin. This is essential for understanding if the model is successfully learning the preferences. Finally, I compared the text generated by the DPO-tuned model against the original reference model to see the change in behavior.

#### Challenges and Key Takeaways

This week was a deep dive into the cutting edge of model alignment.

- **The Power of Direct Optimization:** DPO is a much more straightforward and stable approach to preference tuning than full RLHF. By converting the problem into a simple loss function, it makes alignment more accessible without sacrificing performance.

- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The training process highlighted the sensitivity of DPO to hyperparameters, especially `beta`. The fluctuations in the loss curve suggested that my initial `beta` of `0.3` might not have been optimal. Finding the right balance—learning the preference without deviating too far from the base model's knowledge—is a key challenge in DPO.

- **Generation Quality is Tricky:** The final generation step was a humbling reminder that even with alignment, getting high-quality output is hard. The DPO-tuned model produced empty newlines, likely due to a combination of imperfect training on a small subset of data and a prompt format that wasn't perfectly suited to the model's new alignment. This shows that alignment is not a magic bullet; it's one part of a larger, more complex system.

- **Beyond Supervised Learning:** This week was the perfect follow-up to SFT and LoRA. It demonstrated that getting a model to perform a task is only half the battle. The other half is ensuring its outputs are aligned with human values and preferences. DPO provides a powerful, direct tool for achieving that alignment. It felt like I was moving from just "teaching" the model to "guiding" its behavior.
